About LL-37

LL-37 is a 37-amino acid human antimicrobial peptide belonging to the cathelicidin family, encoded by the CAMP gene. It is naturally expressed in neutrophils, epithelial cells, and macrophages. In research, LL-37 is studied for its direct antimicrobial activity (bactericidal against gram-positive and gram-negative bacteria), wound healing, and immune modulation properties.

LL-37 is sold worldwide as a research-use-only (RUO) compound. Because of its antimicrobial nature, endotoxin (LAL) testing is particularly relevant alongside standard HPLC purity and mass spectrometry — contamination can confound antimicrobial research outcomes. Third-party COA coverage is the key differentiator between suppliers.

LL-37 vs BPC-157 vs KPV

Three peptides studied in wound healing, antimicrobial defense, and tissue-protection research:

  • Origin — LL-37 is a naturally occurring human cathelicidin; BPC-157 is a synthetic gastric pentadecapeptide; KPV is a tripeptide derived from the C-terminal of alpha-MSH
  • Primary mechanism — LL-37: direct antimicrobial disruption of bacterial membranes + immunomodulation; BPC-157: systemic tissue repair, gut lining, tendon/ligament; KPV: anti-inflammatory via NF-κB inhibition, particularly studied in gut models
  • Endotoxin testing relevance — Especially important for LL-37 and KPV research, where bacterial contamination could confound outcomes; Particle Peptides includes endotoxin testing in their COA panel
  • Research stage — BPC-157 has the broadest preclinical literature of the three; LL-37 has substantial in vitro and some in vivo data; KPV is more recent with growing interest in gut research

Frequently Asked Questions

LL-37 is a 37-amino acid human antimicrobial peptide belonging to the cathelicidin family. It is encoded by the CAMP gene and naturally expressed in neutrophils, epithelial cells, and macrophages. In research it is studied for antimicrobial, wound-healing, and immunomodulatory properties.

How is LL-37 different from BPC-157?

About Epithalon

Epithalon (also spelled Epitalon) is a synthetic tetrapeptide (Ala-Glu-Asp-Gly) derived from Epithalamin, a natural extract of the pineal gland. Developed by Russian gerontologist Professor Vladimir Khavinson, it has been studied for telomerase activation, circadian rhythm regulation, and longevity-related endpoints.

Epithalon is sold worldwide as a research-use-only (RUO) compound. Because it is a short tetrapeptide, mass spectrometry sequence verification is particularly important — HPLC alone may not detect single amino acid substitutions. Third-party COAs with MS confirmation are the key signal when comparing suppliers.

Epithalon vs Thymosin Alpha-1 vs MOTS-c

Three short peptides studied in longevity and healthy-aging research — each with a distinct origin and mechanism:

  • Origin — Epithalon is a synthetic pineal-derived tetrapeptide; Thymosin Alpha-1 is a thymic peptide (28 aa); MOTS-c is encoded in mitochondrial DNA — a fundamentally different class
  • Primary research focus — Epithalon: telomerase activation and circadian regulation; Thymosin Alpha-1: T-cell maturation and immune modulation; MOTS-c: mitochondrial biogenesis and metabolic health
  • Peptide length — Epithalon is a tetrapeptide (4 aa); Thymosin Alpha-1 is 28 aa; MOTS-c is 16 aa — mass spectrometry is especially important for the shorter peptides
  • Research depth — Epithalon has extensive Russian-language literature from the Khavinson Institute; Thymosin Alpha-1 has broad Western clinical data; MOTS-c has more recent research from mitochondrial biology

Frequently Asked Questions

What is Epithalon?

Epithalon (also spelled Epitalon) is a synthetic tetrapeptide (Ala-Glu-Asp-Gly) derived from Epithalamin, a natural extract of the pineal gland. It is studied in research for telomerase activation and potential longevity-related endpoints.

What is the difference between Epithalon and Epitalon?

Epithalon and Epitalon refer to the same compound — Ala-Glu-Asp-Gly. The spelling difference is a transliteration artifact from Russian scientific literature. Both names appear on supplier product listings.

What should I look for in an Epithalon supplier?

Look for third-party COAs with both HPLC and mass spectrometry confirming the correct tetrapeptide sequence. Sequence verification via MS is especially important for short peptides where substitutions can be difficult to detect by HPLC alone.

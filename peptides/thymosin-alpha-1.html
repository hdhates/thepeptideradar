About Thymosin Alpha-1

Thymosin Alpha-1 (Tα1) is a naturally occurring 28-amino acid peptide originally isolated from the thymus gland and derived from prothymosin alpha. It is studied in research for immune modulation — specifically T-cell maturation, dendritic cell activation, and antiviral defense mechanisms. It is marketed as Zadaxin in some countries with approved clinical indications.

Thymosin Alpha-1 is not related to Thymosin Beta-4 (TB-500) despite sharing a name — they are entirely different peptides with different mechanisms. As a 28-aa peptide, mass spectrometry sequence confirmation alongside HPLC purity is the key quality signal when comparing research suppliers.

Thymosin Alpha-1 vs Thymosin Beta-4 vs LL-37

Three peptides studied in immune and tissue-defense research — often confused due to naming similarities:

  • Origin — Thymosin Alpha-1 is thymic; Thymosin Beta-4 is an actin-sequestering peptide (unrelated to TA-1 despite the name); LL-37 is a human cathelicidin antimicrobial peptide
  • Primary mechanism — Thymosin Alpha-1: T-cell and dendritic cell modulation; Thymosin Beta-4: actin dynamics, wound healing, angiogenesis; LL-37: direct antimicrobial activity and immunomodulation
  • Research depth — Thymosin Alpha-1 has the most extensive clinical literature of the three, including approved use in several countries; TB-500 and LL-37 are predominantly preclinical
  • Common confusion — Thymosin Alpha-1 and Thymosin Beta-4 are frequently conflated due to naming. They share no structural or mechanistic overlap.
